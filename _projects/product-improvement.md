## Overview

For this product-improvement activity I chose a mug and set out to make it better for the person using it. Rather than jumping straight to a fix, I used the design process end to end: brainstorm broadly, narrow deliberately, and document as I went. The goal was to practice how good improvements actually get made.

## The Problem

A mug is a simple, familiar object, which makes it a good challenge to improve. To enhance something like this you first have to find where it falls short for the consumer, then generate features that solve that problem rather than just adding to it.

## Ideation & Concept Development

I brainstormed a large number of ideas and then narrowed them down to the ones that would be most useful or most appealing. Documenting and sketching ideas while brainstorming helped later in the design process: sketches give a rough sense of where things sit and how they look, and they let group members visualize the same concept. I found that brainstorming with a group was more efficient than working alone, since everyone brings a different perspective and surfaces ideas I would have missed.

## Outcome & Reflection

This activity taught me how the design and brainstorm process works and how to use it while solving problems. The biggest takeaway was the value of generating many ideas before committing, and of capturing them on paper so a team can build on them together.
