## Overview

For one of my first class projects, I designed and 3D printed a set of LEGO bricks to build a miniature McDonald's restaurant. I modeled real LEGO pieces from scratch and invented new ones that don't exist, treating the humble brick as a design system I could extend. The intent was to take everything we'd learned about CAD, sketching, and 3D printing and pull it into a single buildable object.

## The Problem

The brief was open-ended: model many types of LEGO bricks and 3D print them into a finished creation. That meant not just replicating existing parts accurately enough to interlock, but also designing new bricks that would fit the same system while giving me the shapes my build actually needed.

## Research & Insights

The idea landed the morning I grabbed McDonald's before school. I looked online for inspiration and kept coming back to the restaurant's iconic slanted roof as the defining silhouette to capture. That reference shaped the whole build: I split the design into a rectangular restaurant body and a distinct sloped roof.

## Ideation & Concept Development

I worked the concept out through sketches before committing to CAD, then divided the McDonald's into its major parts. Setting goals for myself and breaking the project into smaller pieces was what kept it manageable and moving.

## Design & Development

I modeled the bricks in Fusion 360, recreating pieces that already exist and authoring new ones to fill gaps in the set. The restaurant walls lean on more rectangular pieces, while the roof called for slanted, angled bricks. Each part was then 3D printed so the physical pieces could snap together into the final structure.

## Outcome & Reflection

The project brought together the core skills we'd been learning — concept sketching, Fusion 360 modeling, and 3D printing — into one cohesive build. More than the model itself, it taught me how to set my own goals and split a large, ambitious project into parts I could actually finish.
